üíß Hydrosocial Territories: Mapping Water-Society Relations

Understanding water systems requires more than just modeling hydrological and ecological health‚Äîit requires insight into how water is embedded in political, and territorial dynamics. My work on hydrosocial territories explores water governance, land cover/use, and ecosystem services through spatial analysis and geographic information science.

Hydrosocial territories are spaces where water and society are co-produced‚Äîshaped by management, and ecological processes. Using GIS, remote sensing, and spatial modeling, I investigate how water rights, and modeling of soil moisture and vegetation phenology reshape these territories over time.

Key Areas of Focus:

  • Quantifying the ecosystem service of soil moisture to vegetation greenness
  • Analyzing land use by ecosystem service
  • Integrating socio-political data with ecohydrological models

üå≤ Wildland-Urban Interface & Population Change

As population growth accelerates along the Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI), the intersection of human settlement and natural landscapes becomes a site of critical environmental concern. This project explores spatial patterns of population increase in fire-prone regions, highlighting how land use, urban expansion, and ecological vulnerability intersect.

Using demographic data, land cover classifications, and spatial overlays, I assess WUI growth and its implications for wildfire risk, resource management, and land-use policy.

ü™¶ Oxford Cemetery Headstone Mapping Project

This collaborative mapping effort, in Advanced GIS (GEO 442/542), documents and analyzes the condition of headstones in the Oxford Cemetery using high-resolution drone imagery, ArcGIS Online, and the Field Maps App. I digitized over 100 headstones and went through qaulity assurance of past students submissions.

The dashboard my team designed is an inventory and assessment of the physical condition, legibility, and historical attributes of each grave marker.
